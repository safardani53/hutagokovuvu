Wakefield set to get new stadium - but the deal is not fully agreed yet

July 21, 2024
blog

WAKEFIELD are set to stay at Belle Vue for 2018 – and get a £12 million revamped stadium for 2020.

But if things do not go well over the next six weeks, they could still leave the city and head to Dewsbury.

Plans for a redevelopment of the dilapidated ground, which would see it turned into a modern 10,000-capacity facility, were revealed yesterday.

However, chairman Michael Carter admitted that it could all be ripped up and Trinity could yet move to Dewsbury next year, rather than in 2019 when building work should be taking place.

SunSport understands one of the main stumbling blocks is the amount of matchday revenue, things like food and drink sales, Wakefield Council wants.

And it is believed the club came very close to pulling out and upping sticks.

Carter said: “What they threw on the table asked a lot of questions of our business and how we saw it would have coped under this proposal.

“There were some parts we felt didn’t fit the club and could have been better but we have agreed that the parties will sit down and thrash out the detail.

“We’ve put a six-week deadline on it but we’re 95 per cent certain we’ll be here next year and there’s light at the end of the tunnel that the new stadium is on its way.

“We need to make sure we can raise further revenues on a non-matchday, you can’t survive on just 14 days a year.

“The ultimate aim is to spend the salary cap but we’ve get to get our revenues up to do that. I see no reason why in 2020 we won’t be spending the full cap.

“We are yet to agree all the minute details and have agreed with all parties that this will be finalised by October 31.

“If this proves to be satisfactory, then the club will stay at Belle Vue for 2018 and be in a new stadium as soon as possible.

“All parties involved have worked extremely hard to get to this point and hopefully we will see a brand new ground at Belle Vue in the near future.”

The plan sees Wakefield Council take on the lease of the stadium then raise money to build the project, which will also see residential and entertainment developments.

Once it is built and a development at nearby Newmarket, where Trinity’s new stadium should have been, starts paying for itself, ownership will be transferred to a trust.

Carter added: “It’s a brand new ground but on the old site so I’m sure we’ll maintain a lot of our heritage.

“The plan is the council will buy the lease then raise monies on that to fund the stadium. No-one would be able to sell it from underneath the club’s noses.”
